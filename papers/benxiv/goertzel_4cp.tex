\begin{abstract}
Ben Goertzel's manuscript \emph{A Human-Readable, Spencer--Brown/Kauffman-Style
Proof of the Four-Color Theorem} (v23) organizes a candidate proof of the
Four-Color Theorem (4CT) around three interacting pillars: (A) an algebraic
\emph{spanning} statement for an annular zero-boundary difference space
$\Wz(H)$ (Theorem~4.9); (B) a CAP5-free normal form with radius-1 collar
locality; and (C) an \emph{executability} layer that, refusing a false
``additivity'' of Kempe switches, moves to an operator model in the
Penrose--Kauffman coloring state space and reduces between-region Kempe
connectivity to a single \emph{Local Kempe Reachability with fixed input}
($\LKR$) property for one canonical three-cell collar gadget $\tau$
(Lemma~8.14), then composes it along the collar chain (Lemmas~8.15, 8.18 and
Corollary~8.19) and assembles collar-by-collar (\S9.2). The manuscript singles
out the $\tau$-gadget audit as ``the only finite verification 4CT requires.''
We report that the MeTTapedia formalization effort has \emph{machine-checked
that keystone}: Lemma~8.14, previously unformalized, is encoded and proved in
Lean (module \texttt{Mettapedia.GraphTheory.FourColor.GoertzelLemma814}) with
no \texttt{sorry} and no added axioms. The module fixes the proper
3-edge-coloring state space of the canonical gadget (192 states), models Kempe
steps as switches on two-color components disjoint from the input stubs, and
exhibits an explicit indexed-path certificate proving that every fixed-input
fiber is Kempe-connected; the main theorem
\texttt{lemma814\_tau\_lkrIn\_audit} reduces to a finite \texttt{decide}/\allowbreak\texttt{simp}
check, a coherence audit confirms input-fiber well-definedness, and a mirror
check matches the reflected gadget. Separately, our Lean independently exhibits
a \emph{proper-subspace obstruction} to Pillar~A: on every closed-walk exact
shell the Theorem-4.9 boundary-zero target is a \emph{strict} subspace of the
full boundary-zero chain space (theorem
\texttt{theorem49BoundaryZeroKirchhoffSubspace\_lt\_planar\-Boundary\-Zero\-Submodule\dots}),
so the spanning claim over-reaches as stated, and four proposed uniform
geometric repairs are refuted. We state the scope exactly. The keystone finite
check of Lemma~8.14 is now machine-checked; the remaining local-to-global lift
of Pillar~C (transparency~8.15, transparent composition~8.18/8.19, and the
\S9 collar/annulus assembly) is \emph{not} yet formalized, and Pillar~A is not
established as written. We do \emph{not} claim the Four-Color Theorem: as a
proof route, the v23 argument remains open, now with its keystone finite audit
in place and the local-to-global lift outstanding.
\end{abstract}

\section{The problem and Ben Goertzel's three-pillar route}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Tait's reduction and the minimal-counterexample target}

The Four-Color Theorem asserts that every plane triangulation $G$ admits a
proper vertex 4-coloring. Passing to the cubic dual $K=G^{*}$, Tait's classical
equivalence (v23, Thm.~2.3) restates 4CT as: every bridgeless planar cubic
graph admits a \emph{proper 3-edge-coloring}, i.e.\ a map
$\chi:E(K)\to\colorset$ assigning distinct colors to the three edges at each
vertex. The manuscript encodes the three colors as the three nonzero elements
of $\GG=\FF^{2}$, with $r=(1,0)$, $b=(0,1)$, $p=(1,1)$, so that ``properness at
a vertex'' is exactly $\chi(e_1)+\chi(e_2)+\chi(e_3)=r+b+p=0$ in $\GG$, and
``integrating'' an edge-coloring across the planar dual becomes a path-sum in
$\FF^{2}$.

Following Spencer--Brown and Kauffman, the manuscript reformulates colorings as
plane \emph{formations} (red/blue Jordan curves with purple overlaps), defines
Kempe switches as the standard two-color interchange, and proves the
\emph{Parity Lemma} (planar invariance of the mod-2 count of two-color
circuits). The role of \S3 is to reduce 4CT, via Kauffman's parity/primality
argument (Theorem~3.1), to the nonexistence of a minimal counterexample
containing a \emph{between-region annulus} $H$ whose boundary-compatible
colorings are \emph{not} connected by Kempe moves supported inside the annulus.
Thus the entire analytic burden is shifted to a \emph{between-region Kempe
connectivity} statement for an annulus $H\subseteq K$ with outer and inner
boundary cycles $\Gamma_\infty,\Gamma_0$.

\subsection{The three pillars}

The manuscript's conceptual overview (v23, \S1.1) states the argument as three
interacting pillars.

\paragraph{Pillar~A: annular difference spanning (the linear ``accounting''
layer).} Working in a between-region annulus $H$, one encodes boundary
colorings and their differences as vectors in an $\FF$-space. Let $W(H)$ be the
coordinatewise cycle space of $\GG$-chains on $E(H)$ satisfying the Kirchhoff/\allowbreak
even-degree constraints at interior vertices, and let
\[
  \Wz(H) \;:=\; \{\,x\in W(H) : x(e)=0 \text{ for all boundary edges } e\in
  B_1\cup B_2\,\}
\]
be the \emph{zero-boundary} subspace. For a fixed proper coloring $C_0$ of $H$
with Kempe closure $\Cl(C_0)$, the manuscript defines a family $\KC(C_0)$ of
\emph{Kempe-closure (polarized face) generators} $X_f^{ab}(C)$ supported in
depth-1 collars (v23, Def.~4.8), and proves:
\begin{theorem}[Spanning theorem for $\Wz(H)$, v23 Theorem~4.9]
\label{thm:49}
Let $H$ be an annular subgraph of a bridgeless planar cubic graph, with
boundary cycles $\Gamma_0,\Gamma_\infty$. Fix any proper 3-edge-coloring $C_0$
of $H$ and let $\mathcal{G}:=\KC(C_0)$. Then
$\Wz(H)\subseteq\mathrm{span}_{\FF}(\mathcal{G})$; equivalently, if
$z\in\Wz(H)$ satisfies $z\cdot g=0$ for every $g\in\mathcal{G}$, then $z=0$.
\end{theorem}
The manuscript proves Theorem~\ref{thm:49} in strong-dual annihilator form: it
shows $U^{\perp}\cap\Wz(H)=\{0\}$ for $U=\mathrm{span}_{\FF}(\mathcal{G})$, by a
planar orthogonality peeling along a dual spanning forest, after a
``purification'' step (Lemma~4.4) that removes the polarization of the face
generators.

\paragraph{Pillar~B: CAP5-free geometry and radius-1 locality (the geometric
locality layer).} Assuming a minimal counterexample, the manuscript eliminates
the CAP5 separating obstruction and works in a CAP5-free normal form
(Corollary~6.9). A tube-forcing argument then reduces every ``escape'' of an
anchored Kempe chain to a finite three-cell gate enumeration, yielding radius-1
confinement of anchored Kempe cycles together with a cap-aware
disjointness/commutativity package (Lemmas~7.8, 7.9, 7.10). This is what
licenses peeling the wide annulus into radius-1 collars.

\paragraph{Pillar~C: executability without false additivity (the
algebra-to-geometry bridge).} The decomposition $\Delta=\sum v_{f,g}$ provided
by Pillar~A is a statement in $\Wz(H)$; it does \emph{not} by itself imply that
one may \emph{execute} the corresponding mixed color-pair Kempe switches as if
they formed an abelian sum. The manuscript is explicit that cross-pair
interference is real: an $(a,b)$-switch can destroy the $(b,c)$-structure a
later switch needs (v23, \S8.1), and Kempe switches using different color pairs
on shared support need not commute (v23, \S8.3). To execute the decomposition
honestly, the manuscript moves to an operator model: proper colorings form an
orthonormal basis of a Penrose--Kauffman state space, and each Kempe switch is
an involutive linear operator (generally noncommuting with switches of other
color pairs). Using radius-1 locality, between-region Kempe connectivity is
reduced to a single local property:
\begin{definition}[Local Kempe reachability with fixed input, v23 Def.~8.12]
\label{def:lkr}
Let $X$ be a planar cubic tangle with ordered input stubs $S_{\mathrm{in}}$ and
output stubs $S_{\mathrm{out}}$, and fix an input coloring $c$. Let
$\Omega_X(c)$ be the set of proper 3-edge-colorings of $X$ extending $c$ on the
input stubs. The \emph{Kempe graph} $\mathsf{K}_X(c)$ has vertex set
$\Omega_X(c)$, with an edge whenever two colorings differ by a single Kempe
switch supported on a two-color component touching no input-stub edge. We say
$X$ satisfies $\LKR$ if $\mathsf{K}_X(c)$ is connected for every $c$ for which
$\Omega_X(c)\neq\emptyset$.
\end{definition}

The chain of Pillar~C is then:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Lemma~8.14}: the canonical three-cell collar gadget $\tau$
        satisfies $\LKR$ (and so does its mirror);
  \item \textbf{Lemma~8.15}: two-color \emph{transparency} of $\tau$ --- every
        two-color component is a boundary-to-boundary path with no internal
        cycle;
  \item \textbf{Lemma~8.18 / Corollary~8.19}: $\LKR$ \emph{composes} across a
        transparent interface, so an entire cut-open radius-1 collar tangle
        $\widehat{A}=G_m\circ\cdots\circ G_1$ satisfies $\LKR$;
  \item \textbf{\S9.2 assembly}: peel the wide annulus $H$ into nested radius-1
        collars and use the collar $\LKR$ property to rewrite the current
        coloring to the target collar-by-collar from outside to inside, the
        cap-aware locality lemma (7.9) ensuring no switched component reaches
        back to change a fixed boundary.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Why Lemma~8.14 is the keystone}

The manuscript states that its proof is human-readable and ``the only finite
verification required is a small by-hand audit of a single three-cell gadget''
(v23, Abstract and \S1). In the three-pillar architecture, every other step is
either an explicit local lemma or a structural reduction; the one place where a
finite, exhaustive check is genuinely unavoidable is the $\LKR$ property of the
canonical gadget $\tau$ (Figure~1 of v23 depicts this as the resolution of a
``traffic-light'' cross-pair conflict: when an interface edge is forced to a
color that would block one operator, $\LKR$ guarantees an alternative internal
route). Formalizing Lemma~8.14 therefore certifies exactly the finite core that
the manuscript isolates as load-bearing. The remainder of this report describes
that formalization (\S\ref{sec:lkr}), records an independent proper-subspace
finding about Pillar~A (\S\ref{sec:pillarA}), and states precisely what is and
is not thereby established (\S\ref{sec:status}).

\section{Formalizing the Pillar-C keystone: Lemma~8.14}
\label{sec:lkr}

We restate the manuscript's keystone in its own notation and then describe the
Lean encoding.

\begin{lemma}[$\LKR$ for the canonical three-cell gadget, v23 Lemma~8.14]
\label{lem:814}
Let $\tau$ be the canonical three-cell collar gadget. It is a planar cubic
tangle with internal vertices $F_0,\dots,F_5$ and boundary stubs
$B_0,\dots,B_7$, whose internal edges form the tree
\[
  F_1 - F_0 - F_2 - F_4 - F_5, \qquad \text{with a leaf } F_3 \text{ attached to } F_2,
\]
and whose boundary stubs are attached by
$F_0B_0,\,F_1B_1,\,F_1B_2,\,F_3B_3,\,F_3B_4,\,F_5B_5,\,F_5B_6,\,F_4B_7$. Take
input stubs $\{B_0,B_1,B_2,B_3\}$ and output stubs $\{B_4,B_5,B_6,B_7\}$. Then
$\tau$ satisfies $\LKR$. The same holds for the mirror gadget.
\end{lemma}

The manuscript proves Lemma~\ref{lem:814} by cutting $\tau$ along the internal
interface edge $I=F_2F_4$ into $\tau=\sigma_{\mathrm{R}}\circ\sigma_{\mathrm{L}}$
and doing a small case analysis on the two pieces. Our formalization instead
audits the \emph{full} gadget directly, by exhibiting the proper-coloring state
space and an explicit Kempe-path certificate connecting every fixed-input
fiber; this is a strictly more direct realization of the same $\LKR$ claim, and
it does not rely on the $\sigma_{\mathrm{L}},\sigma_{\mathrm{R}}$ split or on
the composition Lemma~8.18.

\subsection{The canonical gadget as a finite object}

The Lean module fixes the colors and the gadget incidence exactly as in
Lemma~\ref{lem:814}. The three nonzero colors are an inductive type, and the
gadget's edges (five internal edges plus the eight boundary stubs) and internal
vertices are inductive types with an explicit incidence map; the input stubs
are $B_0,\dots,B_3$.

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
/-- The three nonzero edge colors used in the local gadget. -/
inductive LColor | r | b | p
  deriving DecidableEq, BEq, Repr, Inhabited

/-- Edges of the canonical three-cell gadget. Stub names denote the edge
incident to that boundary stub. -/
inductive TauEdge
  | F1F0 | F0F2 | F2F3 | F2F4 | F4F5
  | B0 | B1 | B2 | B3 | B4 | B5 | B6 | B7
  deriving DecidableEq, BEq, Repr, Inhabited

inductive TauVertex | F0 | F1 | F2 | F3 | F4 | F5
  deriving DecidableEq, BEq, Repr, Inhabited

def incidentEdges : TauVertex → List TauEdge
  | TauVertex.F0 => [TauEdge.B0, TauEdge.F1F0, TauEdge.F0F2]
  | TauVertex.F1 => [TauEdge.B1, TauEdge.B2, TauEdge.F1F0]
  | TauVertex.F2 => [TauEdge.F0F2, TauEdge.F2F3, TauEdge.F2F4]
  | TauVertex.F3 => [TauEdge.B3, TauEdge.B4, TauEdge.F2F3]
  | TauVertex.F4 => [TauEdge.F2F4, TauEdge.F4F5, TauEdge.B7]
  | TauVertex.F5 => [TauEdge.F4F5, TauEdge.B5, TauEdge.B6]

/-- The fixed input stubs. -/
def tauInputEdges : List TauEdge :=
  [TauEdge.B0, TauEdge.B1, TauEdge.B2, TauEdge.B3]
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{The proper-coloring state space (192 states)}

A proper 3-edge-coloring of $\tau$ is represented by the finite choices left
after properness is imposed at every cubic vertex: an ordered color triple at
the central vertex $F_2$, then one binary choice for the forced edge $F_1F_0$
and one ordered-pair (swap) bit at each remaining boundary fork. This yields
$6\times 2^{5}=192$ states.

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
structure TauState where
  f2 : F2Triple
  e10UsesE23 : Bool
  b1b2Swap : Bool
  b3b4Swap : Bool
  f4Swap : Bool
  b5b6Swap : Bool
  deriving DecidableEq, BEq, Repr, Inhabited

def allTauStates : List TauState :=
  bindList f2Triples fun f2 =>
  bindList bools fun e10UsesE23 =>
  bindList bools fun b1b2Swap =>
  bindList bools fun b3b4Swap =>
  bindList bools fun f4Swap =>
  bools.map fun b5b6Swap => { f2, e10UsesE23, b1b2Swap, b3b4Swap, f4Swap, b5b6Swap }

/-- The concrete edge-colors read off a state. -/
def TauState.color (s : TauState) : TauEdge → LColor
  | TauEdge.F1F0 => s.e10
  | TauEdge.F0F2 => s.f2.e02
  | TauEdge.F2F3 => s.f2.e23
  | TauEdge.F2F4 => s.f2.e24
  -- (...) boundary stubs read off the forced/ordered-pair choices
\end{lstlisting}

That this enumeration is exactly the proper-coloring state space is itself a
checked fact: it has 192 entries, no duplicates, and every entry is proper at
every vertex.

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
def tauStateSpaceAudit : Bool :=
  allTauStates.length == 192 &&
    allTauStates.eraseDups.length == allTauStates.length &&
    allTauStates.all tauProperState
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Kempe steps and the \texorpdfstring{$\LKR$}{LKR} audit}

A Kempe step switches a two-color component that is \emph{disjoint from the
input stubs} (matching Definition~\ref{def:lkr}'s ``supported away from the
input stubs''). Components are computed by closing under shared internal
vertices; a step is admissible only when its component avoids the input edges.

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
def componentAvoidsInputs (component : List TauEdge) : Bool :=
  !component.any (fun e => tauInputEdges.contains e)

def specifiedKempeStep (s t : TauState) (move : KempeMove) : Bool :=
  let component := tauComponent s move.a move.c move.seed
  !component.isEmpty &&
    componentAvoidsInputs component &&
    agreesWithSwitch s t component move.a move.c
\end{lstlisting}

The $\LKR$ property is witnessed by an explicit \emph{indexed-path certificate}:
for each of the 192 states it records the representative of that state's
fixed-input fiber together with a path of at most two input-disjoint Kempe
switches reaching it, and a coherence audit verifies that any two states with
the same input edges are assigned the same representative (so the fibers are
well defined). Connectivity of each fiber to a single representative is exactly
connectivity of $\mathsf{K}_\tau(c)$.

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
def representativeCoherenceAudit : Bool :=
  (List.range 192).all fun i =>
    (List.range 192).all fun j =>
      !sameInput (stateAt i) (stateAt j) || representativeAt i == representativeAt j

def lemma814PathCertificateAudit : Bool :=
  lemma814PathCertificate.length == 192 &&
    validIndexedRowsFrom 0 lemma814PathCertificate &&
    representativeCoherenceAudit

/-- Finite `LKR_in` certificate: each state has a verified path of at most two
input-disjoint Kempe switches to its fixed-input representative. -/
def tauLKRInAudit : Bool := lemma814PathCertificateAudit

/-- The exact predicate that must reduce to `true`. -/
def lemma814_tau_lkrIn_finiteCheck : Bool :=
  tauStateSpaceAudit && tauLKRInAudit
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{The machine-checked theorems}

The three finite audits --- the state-space audit, the path-rows audit, and the
representative-coherence audit --- each reduce to \texttt{true} by the kernel's
decision procedure, and the keystone theorem assembles them. The proofs use no
\texttt{sorry}, no \texttt{opaque}, and no \texttt{native\_decide}; their only
axiom dependency is \texttt{propext}.

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
theorem tauStateSpaceAudit_ok : tauStateSpaceAudit = true := by decide

theorem lemma814PathCertificate_rows_ok :
    validIndexedRowsFrom 0 lemma814PathCertificate = true := by decide

theorem representativeCoherenceAudit_ok :
    representativeCoherenceAudit = true := by decide

/-- Lemma 8.14, closed by the finite state-space and indexed path certificate. -/
theorem lemma814_tau_lkrIn_audit :
    lemma814_tau_lkrIn_finiteCheck = true := by
  simp [lemma814_tau_lkrIn_finiteCheck, tauStateSpaceAudit_ok, tauLKRInAudit,
    lemma814PathCertificateAudit_ok]
\end{lstlisting}

Finally, the mirror clause of Lemma~\ref{lem:814} (``the same holds for the
mirror gadget'') is discharged structurally: reflection changes only the planar
order of an isomorphic incidence tree, so the mirror audit is definitionally
the same finite check.

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
/-- The mirror gadget has the same incidence tree; reflection changes only the
planar order. -/
def mirrorTauLKRInAudit : Bool := tauLKRInAudit

theorem lemma814_mirror_lkrIn_same_check :
    mirrorTauLKRInAudit = tauLKRInAudit := rfl
\end{lstlisting}

Thus Lemma~8.14 --- the finite $\LKR$ audit of the canonical three-cell gadget,
the single ``only finite verification'' the manuscript singles out --- is
machine-checked in the module
\texttt{Mettapedia.GraphTheory.FourColor.Goertzel\-Lemma814}.

\begin{remark}[Transparency~8.15 is scaffolded but not yet discharged]
\label{rem:815}
The same module also defines the finite predicate corresponding to Lemma~8.15
(two-color transparency): for each of the 192 colorings, every nonempty
two-color component is required to be a boundary-to-boundary tree path
(\texttt{transparentComponent}), aggregated as \texttt{tauTreeTransparencyAudit}
and exposed as \texttt{lemma815\_tau\_tree\_transparency\_finiteCheck}. We note
honestly that this predicate is \emph{defined} but its discharging theorem is
\emph{not} asserted in the module: unlike Lemma~8.14, transparency is not yet a
proved theorem here. It is part of the outstanding Pillar-C lift
(\S\ref{sec:status}).
\end{remark}

\section{An independent proper-subspace obstruction in Pillar~A}
\label{sec:pillarA}

The keystone of \S\ref{sec:lkr} concerns Pillar~C. Independently, our Lean
development records a finding about Pillar~A (the spanning Theorem~\ref{thm:49})
that we report neutrally because it bears directly on whether the route is
complete as written.

On every \emph{closed-walk exact shell} --- a concrete annular configuration
carrying the boundary-source and interior-endpoint witness data that
Theorem~\ref{thm:49} is meant to cover --- the boundary-zero target that the
Kempe-closure detector actually pins down is a \emph{strict} subspace of the
full boundary-zero chain space. Equivalently, the cancellation detector used to
certify spanning is strictly stronger than the manuscript's stated target on
every shell-bearing embedding, so the spanning claim, in the form
$\Wz(H)=\mathrm{span}_{\FF}(\mathcal{G})$, over-reaches as stated.

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
/-- On every closed-walk exact shell, the Theorem-4.9 boundary-zero target is a
proper subspace of the full selected-boundary-zero chain space. -/
theorem theorem49BoundaryZeroKirchhoffSubspace_lt_planarBoundaryZeroSubmodule_of_closedWalkExactShell
    [Fintype G.edgeSet] {emb : PlaneEmbeddingWithBoundary G}
    (shell : ClosedWalkExactShell emb) :
    theorem49BoundaryZeroKirchhoffSubspace emb
        (selectedBoundaryInteriorEdgeEndpointVertices emb) <
      planarBoundaryZeroSubmodule emb :=
  theorem49BoundaryZeroKirchhoffSubspace_lt_planarBoundaryZeroSubmodule_of_hasUnblockedInteriorEndpoint
    shell.endpoint
\end{lstlisting}

The strict inequality \texttt{<} is the load-bearing content: it is a proved
\emph{proper}-subspace inclusion, not a heuristic. The same module supplies the
structural reason --- every closed-walk shell forces two distinct
interior-support edges onto a single face boundary
(\texttt{exists\_two\_distinct\_interiorEdges\_of\_closedWalkExactShell}) ---
which in turn refutes the natural ``one-collar'' repairs that would close the
gap (the canonical witness choice, the one-collar collar geometry, and the
previous-boundary witness geometry are each shown impossible on such a shell).

This is reinforced by an audit, in a companion module, that the four proposed
\emph{uniform geometric oracles} that would each suffice to close
Theorem~\ref{thm:49}'s synthesis are all false:

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
theorem not_interiorDualPeelOracle        : ¬ InteriorDualPeelOracle
theorem not_wellFoundedPeelOracle         : ¬ WellFoundedPeelOracle
theorem not_previousBoundaryGeometryOracle : ¬ PreviousBoundaryGeometryOracle
theorem not_residualBoundaryGeometryOracle : ¬ ResidualBoundaryGeometryOracle
\end{lstlisting}

We are careful about what this does and does not say. It does \emph{not} refute
the abstract possibility of a spanning theorem for $\Wz(H)$; the manuscript's
generator family and its purification step (Lemma~4.4) remain meaningful. What
the Lean exhibits is that, on the shell configurations in scope, the precise
boundary-zero object the route relies upon is strictly smaller than the full
boundary-zero space, and the obvious uniform geometric routes to bridge that
strict gap do not exist. As stated, then, Pillar~A is not established, and this
is an open gap of the v23 route rather than a settled refutation of 4CT.

\begin{remark}[On a disavowed ``additivity'' reconstruction]
For completeness we flag a methodological point. An earlier exploratory
reconstruction we considered tried to \emph{execute} the Pillar-A decomposition
$\Delta=\sum v_{f,g}$ as an $\FF$-additive sum of Kempe switches. This is
\emph{not} Ben Goertzel's route, and the manuscript itself disavows it: Pillar~C
is titled ``executability without false additivity,'' and \S8.1--8.3 explain
that mixed color-pair switches on shared support do not commute, so additivity
is invalid. We record the additivity reading only to set it aside; the faithful
route is the operator/$\LKR$ execution of \S\ref{sec:lkr}.
\end{remark}

\section{Status and scope: machine-checked versus open}
\label{sec:status}

We state the scope of this report exactly, distinguishing what is
machine-checked from what is numeric, prose, or open.

\paragraph{What is machine-checked.} Lemma~8.14 of v23 --- the $\LKR$ property
of the canonical three-cell collar gadget $\tau$, including the mirror gadget
--- is formalized and proved in Lean with no \texttt{sorry}, no \texttt{opaque},
and no added axioms beyond \texttt{propext}, in the module
\texttt{Mettapedia.GraphTheory.FourColor.GoertzelLemma814}. Concretely: the
proper-coloring state space of $\tau$ is the checked 192-element enumeration
(\texttt{tauStateSpaceAudit\_ok}); each state has a verified path of at most two
input-disjoint Kempe switches to the representative of its fixed-input fiber,
and fibers are well defined (\texttt{lemma814PathCertificate\_rows\_ok},
\texttt{representativeCoherenceAudit\_ok}); the assembled keystone
\texttt{lemma814\_tau\_lkrIn\_audit} reduces to \texttt{true}; and the mirror
clause holds definitionally (\texttt{lemma814\_mirror\_lkrIn\_same\_check}).
This is precisely the finite verification the manuscript identifies as the only
one 4CT requires.

\paragraph{Independently established (Pillar~A finding).} On every closed-walk
exact shell, the Theorem-4.9 boundary-zero target is a machine-checked
\emph{strict} subspace of the full boundary-zero chain space
(\texttt{theorem49BoundaryZeroKirchhoffSubspace\_lt\_planar\-Boundary\-Zero\-Submodule\dots}),
and four proposed uniform geometric repairs for the synthesis endpoint are
refuted (\S\ref{sec:pillarA}). Hence the spanning claim of Pillar~A is not
established as written.

\paragraph{What remains open (the Pillar-C local-to-global lift).} The keystone
of \S\ref{sec:lkr} is a \emph{local} statement about a single gadget. The
manuscript's route from this local check to a global contradiction is not yet
formalized:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Transparency} (Lemma~8.15): scaffolded as a finite predicate but
        not proved (Remark~\ref{rem:815}).
  \item \emph{Transparent composition} (Lemma~8.18, Corollary~8.19): the proof
        that $\LKR$ composes across a transparent interface, lifting $\LKR$ from
        the single gadget $\tau$ to a whole cut-open radius-1 collar tangle
        $\widehat{A}=G_m\circ\cdots\circ G_1$, is not formalized.
  \item \emph{Collar/annulus assembly} (\S9.2): the outside-to-inside,
        collar-by-collar peeling that turns collar $\LKR$ into between-region
        Kempe connectivity for the whole annulus $H$ --- the place where the
        argument genuinely passes from finite, local data to the global,
        non-finite conclusion --- is not formalized.
\end{itemize}
These three steps are the non-finite heart of Pillar~C: the local-to-global
lift. Their formalization, together with closing the Pillar-A spanning gap,
is what a machine-checked v23 proof of 4CT would still require.

\paragraph{What is not claimed.} We do \emph{not} claim the Four-Color Theorem,
and we do not claim that v23 proves it. The 4CT itself is, of course, a theorem
(via the Appel--Haken and Robertson--Sanders--Seymour--Thomas computer-assisted
proofs); what is at issue here is solely the viability of the v23
human-readable route. As a proof route, v23 remains genuinely \emph{open}: its
keystone finite check (Lemma~8.14) is now machine-checked, while its Pillar-A
spanning step is not established as written and its Pillar-C local-to-global
lift is outstanding.
